Seminar Report: New York City Poison Control Center
That Is Heated in a Closed Container


Introduction

The Poison Center is an emergency telephone hotline that is available to both the public and health care providers 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. The Poison Center is in a unique position to identify potential public health risks, new drugs of abuse trends, and potential bioterrorism activity. In this presentation, the Ms. Mercurio-Zappala discusses various topics from holiday toxins to what’s new in the news such as the opioid epidemic.

 Body

NYC PPC Organization is part of Department of Health and Mental Hygiene, affiliated with American Association of Poison Control Centers. They have staff of 20 pharmacists, nurses, and PAs. The staff is training in house for over 6 months and national certification. There are also medical backups, including certified medical toxicologists.

The Poison Center serves the public and professionals. NYS had 5 poison centers and 1 education center. In 2011, the state cut funding by 50%. Increase population to cover 12 million residents. Provides 24/7 telephone services to public and professionals. Provide inpatient consultation and care in 3 major hospitals. They also have a weekly outpatient clinic.

Anyone could call the poison center, from both public and health care providers. In 2013, 92321 calls were answered. Exposures mostly were in children. The most common request is from the administration of public health. The most common cause is cosmetic/personal care, analgesics, household cleaners, foreign bodies, topical, vitamins, pesticide/rodenticides, arts/crafts, antibiotics, and antihistamines.  

Anyone calls expected to answer the following questions: name, age, weight; what was ingested and how much; when the ingestion occurred; if the patient has any symptoms. Specialist may make recommendations based on the history of the ingestion and how the patient is at the time of the call. Follow up calls may be performed.

What is a poison? Any substance which may cause harm if swallowed, inhaled or splashed. There are many types of exposures, and signs and symptoms of toxic ingestion including unexplained nausea or vomiting, abdominal cramps etc. What to do? Call the PCC and be prepared to give the information. Management of exposures: gently remove any substances. Wash the skin with mild soap. Inhalation, open all doors and windows, avoid inhaling the fumes, immediately remove the patient to fresh air. Ocular exposures, rinse eyes with room temperature water for 5 minutes.  

The most toxic agents in the acute setting are calcium channel blockers, oral hypoglycemic, iron, oven cleaners/drain cleaners, lamp oil/furniture oil, and anti freeze/windshield wiper fluid.

Bees, spiders and snakes. Keep the victim calm, and wash the area. Plants can kill. Dieffenbachia is toxic if ate.  Christmas Holy leaves are toxic; they are used in folk medicine. Mistletoe berries may cause GI distress. Adulterated tea or concentrated extracts cause hallucination. Poinsettia may cause irritation.

Laundry pods if ingested cause coughing and choking, eye and oral irritation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, drowsiness and lethargy, respiratory distress, unresponsiveness and seizures. Carbon monoxide is toxic. It is odorless, colorless and tasteless. It is not heavier or lighter than oxygen: it mixes well in the atmosphere. Carbon monoxide can pass through the dry wall between rooms. If the flame is orange, it indicates carbon monoxide. If symptoms occur, open windows, leave for fresh air, and go to the emergency department. Treatment is 1000% oxygen.
Summary

In summary, many things are toxic depending on dose. The poison center is available 24/7. There are phone numbers (e.g., 1-800-222-1222) available and answer questions.
